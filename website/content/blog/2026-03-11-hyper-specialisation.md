---
title: "Hyper Specialisation: Why Your AI Agent Shouldn't Know Everything"
date: "2026-03-11"
description: The case for narrow, focused agents over one generalised assistant.
slug: hyper-specialisation
---

The instinct when setting up an AI agent is to give it everything. Your codebase, your emails, your calendar, your reading list, your notes app. The logic seems sound: more context means better answers. In practice, you end up with an agent that does a lot of things adequately and almost nothing well.

The better pattern, once you've hit that ceiling, is specialisation.

A coding agent that only knows your codebase and your conventions. A research agent scoped to your saved articles and reference documents. A communications agent with access to your inbox and nothing else. Each one is leaner. Each one is faster. And each one is far less likely to drag irrelevant context into a decision that doesn't need it.

The gains aren't just about speed. A specialised agent makes fewer category errors. When you ask your coding agent about a function, it's not filtering through six months of email threads to find the answer. When you ask your research agent to summarise a paper, it's not confused about whether you meant the paper or the GitHub issue with a similar title. The context is clean, so the answers are cleaner.

There's also a maintenance argument. A generalised agent accumulates baggage. The more you use it across different domains, the more its memory fills with context that's only sometimes relevant. You end up either pruning it manually or accepting that it's going to occasionally surface something weird from three months ago. Specialised agents don't have this problem — each one stays within its lane because that's all it has.

The objection is usually management overhead. Running three agents sounds like three times the work. But the overhead of managing separate agents — starting them, stopping them, knowing which one to ask — is mostly a tooling problem, not a fundamental one. Once the tooling handles process lifecycle and makes switching between agents instant, the cost drops close to zero.

The more interesting cost is the one you're already paying: a generalised agent that hedges, qualifies, and occasionally gets it wrong because it's carrying too much. That cost is invisible right up until it isn't.

Start with one specialised agent and notice how much faster and cleaner it feels compared to the sprawling generalist you had before. Then add a second. The workflow shifts quickly — instead of one agent you manage carefully, you have a few you barely think about, each doing its specific job without interference.

That's the actual promise of multi-agent setups. Not more power in a single assistant. More clarity from several focused ones.
